How to use this

One reference for a domestic environment run on clinical infection-prevention logic. Consult it by phase on the day, and by material when you meet a surface you are unsure about.

Primary goal

Interrupt transmission

Clean the points that actually carry contamination — hands, touchpoints, wet zones, food contact — rather than cleaning by appearance. Visible tidiness and microbial risk are different problems.

Primary goal

Make it repeatable

A protocol that is followed at 80% every week beats one followed at 100% twice a year. Effort ceilings, fixed cues and pre-staged equipment exist to protect adherence.

The two ways in

By phase. Daily reset every evening, weekly reclean on Saturday morning, deep clean on the first Saturday of the month. Each has a checklist that remembers your progress and clears itself when the interval rolls over.

By material. When you are standing in front of a surface and unsure, go to the materials section. Products map to materials, not to rooms — the zones organize your time, the materials determine the chemistry.

What this is not

Not a standard for a setting in which clinical care is delivered, and not a substitute for regulated healthcare cleaning requirements. It borrows the reasoning — cleaning before disinfection, zoning, single-zone equipment, observed contact time, defined completion criteria — and applies it at domestic scale.

Not sterility. The objective is risk reduction to a level appropriate for a home, at an effort level that is sustainable indefinitely.

Not a licence to exceed the label. Product instructions and safety data sheets govern all agent use, and where a manufacturer's manual and this document disagree, the manual wins.

Revision note

Revision 2.0 consolidates the three-phase protocol, the material matrix, the supply list and the appliance procedures into a single document. Standing principles

These govern every phase and are stated once. Where a step is ambiguous, the principle decides.

01 — Clean, then disinfect

Cleaning removes soil and organic matter through detergent and friction. Disinfection reduces surviving microorganisms on an already-clean surface. The two are sequential, never interchangeable.

A disinfectant applied over organic matter is largely consumed by that matter before it reaches anything living. A surface that has not been cleaned has not been disinfected, regardless of what was sprayed on it. This is the single most common failure in domestic cleaning.

Sterilization is a clinical process and is not indicated anywhere in a home.

02 — Order of operations
  1. Clean to contained. Cleanest zone first, sanitary areas last, in one direction through the residence.
  2. Top to bottom. Surfaces before floors, so displaced soil falls onto what has not yet been cleaned.
  3. Dry to wet. Remove loose debris before introducing liquid. Water on dry soil makes a film that is harder to lift and spreads it further.
  4. Low-touch before high-touch within a zone, so the surfaces that matter most are cleaned with the least-loaded cloth.

Where two rules conflict, the lower number wins.

03 — Contact time

A disinfectant works only while the surface stays visibly wet for the interval on its label. Bio Stain is 30 seconds to 1 minute; 70% alcohol needs roughly 30 seconds. Wipe after the interval has elapsed, not before — and do not chase a dry patch by adding more product to an area you have already wiped.

70% isopropyl outperforms 99% as a disinfectant. Water is needed for alcohol to denature proteins, and it slows evaporation enough to hold contact time. 99% flashes off too fast.

04 — Equipment does not cross zones

Cloths, brushes and bottles stay in the zone they belong to. A cloth goes to laundry at the end of its zone rather than being rinsed and reused, and it is never returned to the solution bottle after touching a surface — double-dipping contaminates the whole container.

Colour-coding failures are invisible until something goes wrong, which is exactly why the assignment has to be fixed rather than remembered.

05 — Assess before you start

Thirty seconds of looking, borrowed directly from clinical practice: is there a spill, breakage, or anything requiring gloves before the routine pass? Is clutter going to make the surface unreachable? Is anything damaged and in need of repair rather than cleaning? Deal with those first, or the routine work will be done around them indefinitely.

06 — Dried deposits are wetted first

Dry brushing or sweeping a dried organic deposit aerosolizes it into your breathing zone. Wet it, cover it, leave it, lift it, bag it, then disinfect and observe the dwell. This applies to bird droppings on the balcony above all.

07 — Balcony containment

The balcony has no drain, and everything on it sits above a neighbouring space. Introduce only the volume of solution a bucket can take back. Wring over the bucket, never over the edge. Carry wastewater indoors for disposal. Nothing is hosed, flooded or swept over the railing.

08 — Hand hygiene

Wash hands at every zone change, and for at least 20 seconds with soap and warm water after any disinfection work or glove removal. Gloves are not a substitute for this; they are a barrier that is itself contaminated by the end of the task.

09 — Stopping on time is adherence

Each phase has an allotted interval. When it elapses, the phase is complete by definition. Work beyond it is outside the protocol and is the mechanism by which routines become unsustainable and then get abandoned.

Kitchen

Wet and dry · blue cloths · highest-priority zone

Contained zone — bathroom, waste

Wet strategy · red cloths · cleaned last, always

  • Red cloths, brushes and bottles live in the bathroom permanently and never leave it.

Making it stick

Design notes for adherence. The protocol is only as good as the number of weeks you actually run it.

Mechanism

Fixed cues, not decisions

Daily reset begins when the workstation shuts down. Weekly reclean is Saturday morning. Deep clean is the first Saturday. Nothing here requires deciding when — only starting.

Mechanism

Effort ceilings

15, 60, 120 minutes. The ceiling is the commitment. Finishing early is fine; running over is a protocol failure, not extra credit.

Mechanism

Pre-staged equipment

Four caddies that never move mean zero setup cost. Setup cost is what kills a routine on a tired evening.

Mechanism

A defined handover

Each daily reset ends with one recorded line: the next session begins with —. Ending at a defined point is what makes the next start cheap.

The minimum viable day

On a day when 15 minutes is not available, do these three things and consider the day covered:

  1. Kitchen containment — food sealed, waste closed, nothing left soaking.
  2. One pass over the touchpoints of whichever zone you used most.
  3. Cloths used today into the laundry.

This is a legitimate outcome, not a partial failure. A protocol with a defined floor survives bad weeks; one without a floor gets abandoned during them.

Organization as infection control

Clutter is not an aesthetic problem in this framework — it is a surface that cannot be reached. Every object sitting on a horizontal surface is a surface that will not be wiped, and every object without a home is a decision to be made again tomorrow.

  • Horizontal surfaces have a defined baseline state. Returning them to it is the daily reset.
  • Anything on the floor blocks the weekly floor pass. Get planters, bins and boxes onto legs or risers.
  • Nothing sits flat on the balcony through the wet months — that is where algae reservoirs form.
  • Reduce products before you reduce anything else. Redundant chemistry is cognitive load disguised as preparedness.
Verification without perfectionism

Clinical programmes verify cleaning by direct observation, fluorescent markers or ATP swabs. The domestic equivalent is the completion criteria — a short, objective list checked once at the end of a phase. Check it, then stop.

Fragranced products are avoided partly for this reason: fragrance masks odour-neutrality, which is one of the few honest signals available for whether a wet zone is actually clean.

Adjacencies & flow

Where rooms meet each other matters more than what is in them. Three adjacencies here work against the zoning, and each has a specific control.

The route

Bedroom → ensuite → living room (library, studio, work) → balcony → kitchen → entryway.

This departs from the standard clean-to-contained order in one place, and deliberately. The ensuite opens off the bedroom rather than off a hallway, so it cannot be left to the end of the pass without walking contaminated equipment back through the clean zone to reach it. It is cleaned immediately after the bedroom instead, as a self-contained unit with its own cloths, and the door stays shut afterwards.

The entryway moves to last for the same reason in reverse: it sits off the kitchen, so cleaning it before the kitchen would drag outdoor soil across food-contact surfaces you had just finished.

Adjacency 01

The bathroom opens into the clean zone

An ensuite puts the most contaminated room in the apartment directly against the least. Ordinary use — humid air, aerosolized droplets from flushing, bare feet crossing the threshold — moves material between them constantly, with no intermediate space to absorb it.

  • Close the lid before flushing. Flushing with the lid up projects aerosol well beyond the bowl, and here it lands in a sleeping area.
  • Run the fan during and for 15–20 minutes after showering, with the door closed. Humidity migrating into the bedroom is what puts mould on the wall the bedroom shares with the bathroom.
  • The bathroom door stays shut, not ajar, except when the fan is clearing the room.
  • The threshold is a boundary: red cloths never cross it, and the bath mat is a bathroom textile, laundered with them and not with bedding.
  • Check the shared wall and the bedroom-side ceiling corner near the door monthly for the first signs of mould.
Adjacency 02

The entryway opens into the kitchen

This is the most consequential feature of the floorplan. Outdoor soil — street grit, whatever is on shoe soles — enters directly into the food-preparation zone with no transition space at all. Coats and shoes are stored a few steps from the counters.

  • Shoes come off at the door, every time, without exception. This single habit does more for the kitchen than any product in the kit.
  • A coarse mat outside the door and an absorbent one inside. The outside mat takes the grit; the inside one takes the moisture.
  • Shoes stored enclosed or on a rack with a tray, never loose on the floor and never on a surface that is also used for anything else.
  • Coats hung, not draped over a kitchen chair. Outerwear carries whatever the day carried.
  • The entry floor is washed weekly regardless of appearance, and is the last floor done in the pass.
  • Keep nothing food-related — no bags of produce, no recycling destined for the kitchen — staged on the entry floor.
Adjacency 03

The balcony opens off the work area, and the library is in the same room

Every trip onto the balcony tracks grit into the work area, and the rare books are in that same room. Books are the least tolerant objects in the apartment and dust is their principal enemy after humidity.

  • A mat at the balcony door, inside, treated as seriously as the entry mat.
  • Balcony footwear stays on the balcony. It does not come in.
  • The balcony door threshold and track are vacuumed weekly with the crevice tool — it is a grit trap and it is directly upstream of the shelves.
  • Keep the balcony door closed on windy days and during any balcony sweeping.
  • Vacuum the work area before dusting the shelves, not after, so you are not settling floor dust onto books you have just cleaned.

Dry, minimal, first

Nothing lives in this room. Cloths arrive clean and leave with the laundry. This is what keeps it a clean zone.

Daily
  • Bed made, surfaces returned to their baseline. That is the whole daily task here.
Weekly — about 7 minutes
  1. Strip the bed. Launder bedding at the highest temperature the label tolerates. Do not shake the bedding out — it aerosolizes everything you are trying to remove.
  2. Dust top to bottom: light fixture, tops of wardrobe and shelves, headboard, nightstand, window sills, then baseboards.
  3. Vacuum, including under the bed and along the edges with the crevice tool. Edge dust is most of the dust.
  4. Alcohol on a green cloth for the touchpoints: door handle both sides, light switch, lamp switch, nightstand surface, phone charger.
  5. Remake with clean bedding.
Monthly
  • Wash the floor. The one wet task in this zone — laminate rules apply: mist the pad, never the floor, dry immediately.
  • Vacuum the mattress surface and rotate it. Launder the mattress pad and pillow protectors with the textile cycle.
  • Curtains down and laundered; rehung slightly damp so creases fall out.
  • Under-bed area cleared and cleaned properly, and the closet floor perimeter.
  • Wardrobe and drawer interiors wiped, and aired.
  • Check the wall shared with the ensuite, and the ceiling corner nearest the bathroom door, for early mould.
Windows
  1. Vacuum the track and the sill with the crevice tool before anything wet. Track grit turns to grey paste the moment it is wetted.
  2. Glass: Windex Vinegar on flat-weave, buffed dry. Not in direct sun.
  3. Frames and sills: Dawn on a damp cloth, then dried.
  4. Check for condensation at the base of the glass and in the corners — in a bedroom, that is the earliest warning of a humidity problem, and it appears here before it appears anywhere else.
  5. Wipe the track dry. Standing water in a window track is a mould site.
What not to do here

No disinfectant spraying as a routine. No air fresheners — they mask the odour-neutrality signal used as a completion criterion. No storing cleaning products in the room. No fabric softener on bedding, which coats fibres and matters for anyone with skin sensitivity.

Ensuite bathroom

Contained zone · red cloths · wet strategy · full-height. Cleaned second in the pass because of where it sits, then closed.

Why this room is cleaned second, not last

Standard order puts the bathroom at the end. That assumes it opens off a hallway. This one opens off the bedroom, so reaching it last would mean carrying red equipment back through the cleanest room in the apartment. Instead it is done immediately after the bedroom, as a sealed unit — gloves and red cloths on entry, everything reprocessed on exit, hands washed, door closed. The zone boundary is the doorway, and it holds in both directions.

Supplies — permanently resident

The bathroom caddy never leaves this room: Dawn · Bio Stain spray · Bar Keeper's Friend · soft nylon tub brush · detail brush · toilet brush and holder · red cloths · nitrile gloves · paper towel · bin with liner.

Store the caddy below the level of any surface used for personal items, and keep nothing in it that is also used elsewhere.

Daily
  • Sink wiped. Counter cleared.
  • Squeegee or towel the tub and any glass after showering — this does more against mildew than any product.
  • Fan on during the shower and for 15–20 minutes after, door closed.
  • Lid down before flushing. Always, but especially here.
  • Towels hung spread out, not folded, so they dry fully.
Weekly — about 12 minutes, in this order
  1. Gloves on. Fan running. Clear the surfaces and remove the bath mat for laundering.
  2. Light fixture and mirror. Mirror: Windex Vinegar on flat-weave, never letting liquid run to the bottom edge where it wicks behind the silvering.
  3. Shelves, cabinet fronts, and the top of the toilet tank.
  4. Sink, tap and counter: Dawn first, then Bio Stain with full dwell. Bar Keeper's Friend on the porcelain when needed.
  5. Tub and surround: Dawn on a soft cloth or the soft nylon brush only. No abrasives on gelcoat. Bio Stain, dwell, rinse.
  6. Shower controls, door handle, light switch — genuine touchpoints, disinfected.
  7. Toilet, second to last. Paper towel only, straight to the bin. Bowl, then seat and lid, then tank, then base, then the floor immediately around it. Bio Stain on the seat plastic, never alcohol.
  8. Floor last, working from the far corner toward the door.
  9. Cloths bagged for the red laundry load. Brushes rinsed and left to dry bristles down. Gloves off, hands washed 20 seconds. Door closed.
Monthly
  • Grout worked with the detail brush; percarbonate with a 15-minute dwell where stained.
  • Drain covers off, mechanical removal with the hook, then Bio-Clean overnight with no water use after.
  • Extractor fan cover removed and washed. Confirm the fan actually moves air — hold a tissue to it.
  • Shower head and taps descaled with citric acid, 15 minutes maximum on chrome, rinsed and dried.
  • Caulking and the baseboard junction inspected. This is where the previous MDF damage happened, so look before it recurs.
  • Cabinet interiors wiped and checked for damp.
  • All bathroom textiles laundered with the textile cycle, separately from bedding.
Material reminders

Fibreglass tub — the most fragile surface in the apartment. No Bar Keeper's Friend, no scouring powder, no green pads. Toilet seat plastic — no alcohol. Chrome — no abrasives, and no acid left on longer than 15 minutes.

Living room

Work area, studio area, library area · clean zone, dry strategy · green cloths, indoor set. Three functions with different requirements in one space.

Order within the room

Dust high → vacuum the floor → then the library → then work and studio surfaces → touchpoints last. The library goes after the floor vacuum, not before, so you are not settling airborne floor dust onto shelves you have just cleaned. This is the one room where the top-to-bottom rule needs a stated exception.

Supplies

Green cloths (indoor set) · alcohol spray · extendable duster · soft natural-bristle brush for books · vacuum with upholstery, brush and crevice tools · fine mesh or cheesecloth for the vacuum nozzle · flat-weave cloths for screens and glass · Windex Vinegar for windows.

Library area

The most conservative protocol in the apartment. Monthly, not weekly.

  • Never apply liquid of any kind to a book. No alcohol, no soap, no leather dressing without advice specific to that binding.
  • Weekly: dust the shelf edges and the tops of the books with a dry cloth or the duster. That is all.
  • Monthly: books out in sections. Hold each closed and firmly shut, dust from the spine outward toward the fore-edge so dust travels away from the text block, using a soft brush or the vacuum on its lowest setting with mesh over the nozzle.
  • Wipe the empty shelf with a barely damp cloth and let it dry completely before books return.
  • Remove a book by gripping the middle of the spine, never by hooking the headcap.
  • Do not pack shelves tight — air needs to move, and tight packing damages bindings on removal.
  • Annually: inspect for foxing, mould odour and insect activity. Isolate anything affected immediately.
  • Keep the collection out of direct sun, off exterior walls, and away from the balcony door. Stable humidity matters more than any cleaning step.
Work area
  • Daily: clear the desk to its defined baseline and record the one-line handover.
  • Weekly: alcohol on a cloth for keyboard, mouse, tablet, chair arms, and the underside edge of the desk. Applied to the cloth, never sprayed at the device.
  • Screens: dry flat-weave only. No alcohol, no Windex, nothing wet.
  • Monthly: cords and cable runs, which are dust traps; behind the monitor; the chair base and castors.
  • This area borders the balcony door — vacuum the threshold and track weekly with the crevice tool.
Studio area
  • Daily: return to a neutral baseline — clear session materials, reset seating and cushions, place one intentional object, shift lighting from overhead to lamp.
  • Weekly: dust, vacuum including upholstery seams, and disinfect the touchpoints a visitor would use — door handle, light switch, chair arms, side table.
  • Monthly: cushion covers and throws laundered with the textile cycle; upholstery seams vacuumed properly; upholstered light fixtures done with the upholstery brush at reduced suction after switching off at the wall and letting them cool.
  • Keep the products used here fragrance-free. A neutral-smelling room is part of what the space is for.
Windows and general
  • Windows as in the bedroom: vacuum the track dry first, Windex Vinegar on flat-weave, frames and sills with Dawn, everything dried.
  • Weekly touchpoints across the room: light switches, door frames, remotes, handles.
  • Monthly: baseboards, vents, curtain rails; curtains laundered; floor washed.

Kitchen

Blue cloths · wet and dry · highest-priority zone, cleaned after the balcony and before the entryway. Granite counters, table and seating, storage closet, all large appliances.

Why this room outranks the bathroom

The bathroom carries the more obvious soil. The kitchen carries the higher consequence: foodborne transmission and cross-contamination. It is also the room the entryway opens into, which means outdoor soil arrives here first. It gets daily attention that no other room gets.

Supplies — kitchen caddy, does not travel

Dawn · alcohol spray · Bio Stain spray · Bar Keeper's Friend · blue cloths · one flat-weave cloth for granite buffing · soft cloth for the cooktop · ceramic scraper · paper towel, for oven cleaning only · bin liners.

Daily
  1. Clear counters completely, then wash with Dawn, then disinfect the food-contact areas with full dwell.
  2. Granite: buff dry with the flat-weave afterwards. On dark polish this is most of the visual result.
  3. Sink cleared, washed, dried. Treat it as a contained item, not a clean one.
  4. Dishes to the dishwasher or rinsed and stacked. Nothing left soaking.
  5. Food sealed and stored. Waste closed.
  6. Table cleared and wiped — Dawn, then disinfect, since it is a food-contact surface.
  7. Tap handle and fridge handle — the two highest-touch points in the apartment.
Weekly — about 12 minutes
  1. Clear surfaces completely, including what has accumulated on the counters through the week.
  2. Cabinet fronts at hand height, appliance handles, oven and cooktop controls.
  3. Glass cooktop: Dawn on a warm, not hot, surface with a soft cloth. No abrasives. Ceramic scraper held near-flat for residue.
  4. Sink: Bar Keeper's Friend with the grain, rinsed completely, dried. Drain and strainer separately — the dirtiest point in the zone.
  5. Table and chairs, including the seat undersides and chair backs, which are handled constantly and cleaned rarely.
  6. Cutting boards: wood by hand and air-dried upright; plastic to the dishwasher.
  7. Dishwasher filter checked and rinsed. Fridge gasket edge wiped.
  8. Bins washed, including the inner rim under the liner.
  9. Floor last, after all surfaces.
Storage closet

Monthly, and the place where problems go unnoticed longest

  • Shelves cleared a section at a time and wiped with Dawn on a damp cloth, then dried completely.
  • Check dry goods for pantry moths and weevils — decanting flour, rice and grains into sealed containers prevents almost all of it. Look at seals and lids, not just contents.
  • Rotate stock forward; check dates; remove anything expired rather than moving it back.
  • Check the floor of the closet and the back corners for spills, crumbs and any sign of pest activity.
  • Leave the door open periodically to air it. An enclosed closet next to a kitchen accumulates humidity, and humidity in a food store is the actual risk.
  • Nothing goes back in damp.

        Selection rule

        One product per surface, rinse between. If you are reaching for a second agent on the same surface in the same pass, stop and check whether the first one simply needed more contact time or more friction.

        Properties that matter

        Clinical guidance selects disinfectants on rapid action at short contact time, effectiveness in the presence of residual organic matter, material compatibility, non-flammability, and stability at working dilution. The Bio Stain scores well on the first three and is the reason it is the working disinfectant here rather than the alcohol, which is faster to evaporate and flammable.

        Neutral-pH detergents (pH 6–8) are preferred for general cleaning because of their breadth of material compatibility. Dawn fits this.

        Hazards & never-mix

        The damage that would actually be done, ranked, plus the combinations that are dangerous rather than merely ineffective.

        Never combine
        • Chlorine bleach + ammonia — chloramine gas
        • Chlorine bleach + any acid, including vinegar and citric acid — chlorine gas
        • Chlorine bleach + alcohol
        • Hydrogen peroxide + acid
        • Simplest rule: one product per surface, rinse between.

        Ventilate during all disinfection work. Keep alcohol away from the range — it is flammable. Store everything in original or clearly labelled, dated containers, and keep safety data sheets accessible.

        The six real hazards, ranked by damage
        1. Water on laminate floors. The core is compressed fibreboard. Swelling at the seams is permanent and unfixable. Damp mop only, dry immediately, and no steam ever.
        2. Alcohol inside the fridge. Causes stress cracking in ABS and polystyrene liners. Damage appears weeks later and spreads. It also dries and cracks door gaskets.
        3. Bar Keeper's Friend on the fibreglass tub. Micro-scratches the gelcoat. Dulled gelcoat holds soil worse, so you scrub harder, and it accelerates. Irreversible.
        4. Bar Keeper's Friend on polished granite. The abrasive dulls the polish and the oxalic acid degrades the sealer. Dark polished stone shows dulling more than any other surface here.
        5. Full-strength percarbonate and a stiff brush on the balcony. Lifts aged paint and abrades the non-slip texture. Half strength, medium nylon brush, test a corner first.
        6. Chlorine bleach on stainless. Chloride attacks the passive chromium oxide layer — pitting and permanent staining. You do not own bleach; keep it that way.

        Glass — panels, railing, door

        Weekly, both faces monthly

        1. Dust dry first, always. Outdoor glass carries grit, and washing over grit is what puts fine scratches into it. A dry flat-weave cloth or the vacuum brush head, top to bottom.
        2. Wash with a few drops of Dawn in warm water on a cloth or a soft applicator. Windex Vinegar is for finishing, not for lifting outdoor soil — it has no detergency and will smear it.
        3. Squeegee in overlapping passes, wiping the blade on a cloth between each. Work top to bottom, finishing at the base.
        4. Finish with Windex Vinegar on flat-weave for clarity, or buff dry if it is already clear.
        5. Dry the base channel and the frame. Water pooling in the shoe of a glass balustrade sits against the fixings, and that is where corrosion starts.

        • No abrasives, no blades, no scouring pads on glass. A tempered panel that gets scratched cannot be polished out.

        Wooden bench

        Weekly wipe, monthly clean, finish renewed seasonally if oiled

        Outdoor wood is the one material here that is damaged more by routine over-cleaning than by neglect. Treat it gently and it will outlast the paint on the floor.

        • Weekly: dry green cloth, or barely damp for marks. Wipe with the grain. Dry it — do not leave it wet.
        • Monthly: Dawn in warm water on a cloth or soft brush, with the grain, then a clear-water wipe, then dried. Get into the slat gaps where debris and moisture collect, and check the underside and the feet, which is where rot begins.
        • Never leave standing water on it, and do not let it sit in a puddle during the floor wash. Move it, or work around it and dry the feet.
        • Avoid: percarbonate at any strength — it bleaches and raises wood grain; pressure washing; chlorine bleach; stiff brushes across the grain; alcohol; and film-forming polishes.
        • Grey weathering is UV, not dirt, and scrubbing will not reverse it. If you want the colour back, that is a sanding and refinishing job, not a cleaning one.
        • Raise it on feet or pads so air moves underneath. Wood sitting flat on damp concrete is the commonest way outdoor furniture fails.

        The right monthly routine depends on the finish — oiled, varnished, painted, or bare. Oiled wood needs re-oiling seasonally and hates film products; varnished wood needs the film kept intact and hates abrasion; bare wood needs the least intervention of all. Confirm which it is and this section can be made specific.

        Potted plants

        Both a hygiene item and a constraint on everything else out here

        The plants are the reason several products cannot be used on this balcony at all. They are also a genuine reservoir — saucers, soil surfaces and pot undersides hold standing water, algae and insect activity.

        Weekly

        • Empty and rinse every saucer. Standing water in a saucer breeds mosquitoes in as little as a week and is the single highest-value plant task here.
        • Remove fallen leaves and spent flowers from the soil surface and from the floor around each pot — decaying material is what feeds fungus gnats and surface mould.
        • Dust the leaves with a soft cloth and plain water only. Dust on foliage genuinely reduces the light a plant gets. No leaf shine products, no soap, no alcohol.
        • Wipe pot exteriors with a damp green cloth.
        • Lift or slide each pot and clean underneath. The ring of trapped damp under a pot is where algae starts and where the floor paint gets marked.

        Monthly

        • Move all plants off the balcony, or well clear, before any washing or percarbonate work begins.
        • Wash saucers properly with Dawn, rinse, dry.
        • Inspect soil surfaces for mould or algae — usually a sign of overwatering or poor airflow. Let the top layer dry between waterings rather than treating it.
        • Check drainage holes are clear.
        • Return everything onto risers, not flat.
        Plants constrain the chemistry
        • Percarbonate, citric acid and any disinfectant must not reach soil, foliage or root runoff. Move the plants first; do not work around them.
        • Watering runoff must be contained the same way cleaning water is — it cannot cross the balcony to a neighbouring space.
        • Confirm nothing here is toxic to any pet with balcony access.
        Algae protocol — revised for paint
        1. Sweep completely dry first. Never wet-dust.
        2. Move everything off — planters, mats, furniture. The algae reservoirs live underneath.
        3. Test first: percarbonate solution on a hidden corner, 20 minutes, check for paint softening or colour lift.
        4. Mix at half strength — roughly ½ cup percarbonate per gallon of warm water.
        5. Pre-wet, apply, dwell 20–30 minutes keeping the surface visibly wet.
        6. Medium nylon brush. Not stiff, not wire.
        7. Rinse thoroughly — residue in the non-slip texture reduces grip, which is the surface's actual job.
        8. Squeegee toward the drain path. Bucket, not hose.
        9. Leave damp rather than wet; let sun and air finish it.
        Droppings

        Never dry-brush or sweep — it aerosolizes the material into your breathing zone. Moisten with detergent solution, cover with paper towel for five minutes, lift and seal in a bag, then disinfect and observe the dwell interval. Gloves throughout.

        Avoid
        • Pressure washing — strips paint from concrete
        • Chlorine bleach — runoff onto neighbours and plants
        • Wet & Forget — leaves a residue film on a surface whose entire job is traction
        • Solvents, stiff brushes, wire brushes
        Between treatments

        Algae returns every wet season. Reduce the recurrence rather than fighting it: keep the drain path clear, get planters, bench and mats onto risers so air moves underneath, and leave nothing sitting flat through the wet months.

        Outdoor dust also returns continuously, and the weekly sweep and vacuum are what hold it. That is maintenance working as intended, not a treatment failing. Judge the balcony against the completion criteria — no visible soil in what this pass addressed, surfaces dry, equipment reprocessed — and not against the interior on the same afternoon.

        4. Wipe out completely, then rinse with clear water on fresh towel until no product remains. Residue produces fumes on the next heat cycle.
        5. Door glass: Dawn and water on a cloth. Keep cleaner out of the vent slots at the base of the door.

        If the unit has a self-clean cycle, it is an alternative to chemical cleaning, never a follow-up to it. Remove racks unless the manual says they are self-clean safe, and ventilate throughout.

        Electrolux stainless refrigerator

        Stainless exterior · plastic and glass interior · monthly, with quarterly coil service.

        Two absolute rules

        No alcohol inside — it causes stress cracking in the liner and dries out the gaskets. No chlorine bleach on the exterior — chloride pits stainless irreversibly.

        Exterior
        • Work along the grain of the steel, never across and never in circles.
        • Dawn and warm water on damp microfiber; a second cloth with clean water; a third dry cloth to buff.
        • The handle is among the highest-touch points in the kitchen — disinfect it after washing.
        • No abrasive pads, no steel wool, no glass cleaner on a brushed finish.
        Interior — monthly
        • Empty one shelf at a time so contents stay cold.
        • Remove glass shelves and let them reach room temperature before washing — cold glass in hot water cracks.
        • Dawn and warm water, or baking soda in warm water for odour. Rinse, then dry.
        • Disinfect only where genuinely indicated, such as a raw meat leak: wash first, Bio Stain, full rinse.
        • Gaskets: fold back the flexible lip and clean the channel with soapy water on a cloth or soft brush, then dry thoroughly. A dirty or dried-out gasket is the usual cause of a poor seal.
        • Crisper drawers removed, washed, dried completely before returning.
        • An open box of baking soda for odour, replaced quarterly. Not a spray.
        Quarterly
        • Pull the unit out; vacuum the condenser coils and the floor beneath with a brush attachment.
        • Clean the drip pan if accessible.
        • Confirm the drain hole at the back of the cabinet is clear.

        Bosch dishwasher

        Stainless interior · weekly filter check, monthly service.

        Filter — the single most important task
        1. Remove the lower rack. Twist the cylindrical fine filter counter-clockwise and lift it out together with the flat mesh filter beneath.
        2. Rinse both under running water; soft brush and a little Dawn on the mesh. Never a wire brush — it damages the mesh and the sealing surface.
        3. Wipe the sump recess clear of debris and glass fragments before reseating.
        4. Refit the flat filter first, then lock the cylinder clockwise until it seats. An unseated filter recirculates soil and is the usual cause of poor wash results and odour.
        Spray arms — monthly
        • Detach upper and lower arms, check each jet for blockage, clear with a toothpick or thin wire.
        • Rinse, refit, confirm they rotate freely.
        Interior and door — monthly
        • Wipe the door gasket and the channel it sits in — this is where biofilm and odour concentrate. Dry after wiping.
        • Clean the detergent dispenser flap and the door edge above the gasket, which the wash cycle never reaches.
        • Run an empty hot cycle with citric acid solution for limescale and residue. No chlorine bleach in a stainless interior.
        • Leave the door ajar after each cycle. Bosch condensation-drying models hold moisture longer than heated-dry machines, which makes this more important, not less.
        Exterior
        • Stainless front along the grain, as with the fridge.
        • Handle and control panel disinfected with alcohol on microfiber — a genuine touchpoint.

        Blomberg washing machine

        Front loading. Attention after every load; service monthly.

        Why this matters more than it looks

        A washer harbouring biofilm recontaminates every textile passed through it, including the cleaning cloths the whole zoning system depends on. This appliance sits upstream of the entire protocol.

        After every load
        • Wipe the door gasket dry. Fold back the rubber lip and remove trapped water, lint and objects.
        • Leave the door and the dispenser drawer ajar. Front loaders develop odour and mould at the gasket almost entirely because they are closed while damp.
        Weekly
        • Remove the dispenser drawer entirely; wash it and its housing with warm soapy water and a soft brush; dry and refit.
        Monthly
        • Run a tub-clean or the hottest available cycle with citric acid solution, drum otherwise empty. No detergent.
        • Open the drain pump filter at the base — place a shallow tray and towel first, as it holds standing water. Unscrew, clear lint and debris, rinse, reseat squarely so it seals.
        • Wipe exterior, control panel and door glass; alcohol on the handle and controls.
        Avoid
        • Excess detergent — the leading cause of residue and odour in front loaders. Use less than feels right.
        • Chlorine bleach on the gasket — degrades the rubber and shortens its life.
        • Fabric softener on anything, and on microfiber above all.

        Blomberg dryer

        Front loading. Every load, plus a monthly service.

        Every load, without exception

        Clear the lint filter by hand. This is a fire-prevention task, not a performance one, and it is the only step in this document with that character.

        Monthly
        • Wash the lint filter in warm soapy water with a soft brush. Fabric softener and dryer sheets leave an invisible film that restricts airflow — this is why a filter that looks clear can still be blocked.
        • Dry the filter completely before refitting.
        • Vacuum the filter housing with the crevice tool.
        • Wipe the drum with a cloth dampened with water only, or alcohol if residue is present, and leave the door open until dry.
        • Exterior and controls as with the washer.
        Depending on model type
        • Heat pump or condenser: empty the water reservoir after each load; remove and rinse the condenser unit monthly per the manual. A clogged condenser presents as long drying times before it presents as anything else.
        • Vented: disconnect and clear the duct at least annually; confirm the exterior vent flap moves freely.

        Confirm which type this unit is and delete the other line — it changes the monthly task list materially.

        Odour-neutral. No detectable residue from cleaning agents.

        Illness & spills

        Escalation and de-escalation, adapted from clinical outbreak procedure. Temporary by design.

        When someone in the household is unwell
        • Touchpoint disinfection moves from weekly to twice daily: door handles, light switches, taps, flush handle, fridge handle, kettle, remotes, phone.
        • The bathroom is cleaned daily rather than weekly, with full dwell time observed on every surface.
        • Hand towels become single-user or are replaced with paper. Bath and hand towels laundered daily.
        • Waste from the unwell person's room is bagged and closed before it leaves the room.
        • Bedding laundered on the hottest cycle the fabric tolerates, and not shaken out before it goes in — shaking aerosolizes.
        • Ventilate every room daily for five to ten minutes.
        • Where an actual kill claim matters — gastrointestinal illness above all — check the Bio Stain label for the specific organism, and observe the longer dwell time it states for that claim rather than the general one.

        De-escalate 48 hours after symptoms resolve: one full weekly reclean at escalated thoroughness, then return to the standard cadence. Escalation is temporary by design — an indefinite one becomes the new baseline and is not sustainable.

        Spill response — blood, vomit, or other body fluids
        1. Gloves on before anything else. Ventilate.
        2. Contain the area. Keep others out of it.
        3. Absorb and remove visible matter with disposable material working inward from the edges, so the spill is not spread outward.
        4. Seal that material in a bag immediately and close it.
        5. Clean the area with detergent.
        6. Disinfect, keeping the surface visibly wet for the full label interval.
        7. Dispose of gloves, then wash hands for at least 20 seconds.
        8. Anything reusable that contacted the spill is laundered hot and separately, or discarded.

        Never dry-wipe or sweep a dried deposit — wet it first. On laminate, work quickly and dry the area completely; the water rule still applies during a spill response.

        Waste
        • Bins are among the highest bioburden items in the apartment and belong in the weekly pass, including the inner rim under the liner.
        • Liners closed before removal, not carried open through clean zones.
        • Kitchen and bathroom bins are separate and their contents never combined indoors.
