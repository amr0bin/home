How to use this

This is the lookup guide, not the working checklist. Open it when you're unsure about a surface — not when you're starting a clean.

Do the work from the Card

Daily, weekly and monthly routines live in the Card. It's one page and it's deliberately short. This document exists so the Card can stay short.

Three ways in
  • Search. Type the material or product into the box above. Fastest route by a wide margin.
  • Surfaces A–Z. Every material in this apartment, in the same four-line format: clean, sanitize, avoid, why.
  • Rooms and appliances. When the question is "what does this room need" rather than "what does this surface take".
One convention

Anything marked unconfirmed depends on a fact about your apartment I don't have. Those entries give the safest option that works regardless, and the full list is under Unconfirmed. Everything not so marked is something I'm confident about for your specific space.

The rules that matter

Eight principles. Everything else in this document is an application of one of them.

Method
  1. Clean, then disinfect. Disinfectant on top of soil is consumed by the soil. A surface that hasn't been washed hasn't been disinfected, whatever you sprayed on it.
  2. Dry before wet. Lift loose debris before any liquid. Water on dry soil makes a film that's harder to remove.
  3. Top to bottom, clean room to dirty room. One exception: in the living room, vacuum the floor before dusting the shelves, so floor dust doesn't settle on books you've just cleaned.
  4. Contact time is real. The surface stays visibly wet for the label interval or the product didn't work. Bio Stain: one minute. Alcohol: thirty seconds.
Containment
  1. Cloths don't cross zones. One colour per room, retired to laundry rather than rinsed and reused.
  2. Never mix products. One product per surface, rinse between.
  3. Nothing leaves the balcony over the edge. No drain, neighbours below. Extract what you introduce.
  4. Prevention beats cleaning. Shoes off, lid down, fan on, surfaces dried. These outperform every product in the kit.
And a definition

Clean enough = no visible soil in the rooms you addressed, surfaces dry, cloths in the laundry. That's the finish line. It exists so there's something to reach.

Surfaces — Books to Glass

Same format throughout. Search is faster than scrolling.

Books, including rare Living room Most fragile

Clean
Dry only. Soft natural-bristle brush, or vacuum on lowest setting with fine mesh over the nozzle. Hold the book closed and firmly shut, brush from spine outward toward the fore-edge.
Sanitize
Never. Not applicable to books.
Avoid
Any liquid at all — no alcohol, no soap, no leather dressing without advice for that specific binding. Never hook the headcap to pull a book out; grip the middle of the spine.
Cadence
Shelf edges weekly. Books themselves monthly, a section at a time. Full inspection annually.
Why
Brushing toward the fore-edge carries dust away from the text block instead of into it. Stable humidity matters more than any cleaning step — keep them off exterior walls, out of sun, away from the balcony door.

Bench, wooden outdoor Balcony Unconfirmed finish

Clean
Dry cloth weekly, or barely damp for marks. Monthly: Dawn in warm water on a cloth or soft brush, with the grain, then a clear-water wipe, then dried. Into the slat gaps; check underside and feet.
Sanitize
Not routinely. Bio Stain on the seat surface only if needed, then wiped.
Avoid
Percarbonate at any strength — bleaches and raises grain. Pressure washing. Bleach. Stiff brushes across the grain. Alcohol. Film-forming polishes.
Cadence
Weekly wipe, monthly clean, finish renewed seasonally if oiled.
Why
Outdoor wood is damaged more by over-cleaning than neglect. Grey weathering is UV, not dirt — scrubbing won't reverse it. Move the bench clear before any percarbonate work on the floor.

Chrome fixtures Bathroom · Kitchen

Clean
Dawn on microfiber, rinse, dry to a shine. The drying is what keeps chrome looking maintained.
Sanitize
70% alcohol. Safe and effective on taps and handles.
Avoid
All abrasives — scratches in chrome cannot be polished out. Acid left longer than 15 minutes.
Limescale
Cloth soaked in citric acid solution wrapped around the fitting, 15 minutes maximum, rinse thoroughly, dry.
Why
Chrome is a thin plating over a softer base. Everything about its care follows from that.

Concrete, painted non-slip Balcony floor

Clean
Sweep dry first, always. Then mist Dawn solution by section, dwell 10 min, brush in two directions with the medium nylon deck brush, extract. Full method under Balcony floor.
Sanitize
Bio Stain on the seated area and touchpoints, full dwell.
Avoid
Pressure washing and chlorine bleach — both strip the paint. Full-strength percarbonate with a stiff brush. Wet & Forget, which films a surface whose job is traction. Any runoff over the edge.
Cadence
Weekly sweep and vacuum. Monthly wash. Algae treatment seasonally in the wet months.
Why
The texture holds soil directionally, so one brush direction cleans half of it. It will never reach an indoor finish — that's the surface doing its job, not a technique failure.

Curtains & textiles All rooms

Clean
Tide Free & Clear, hottest temperature the label tolerates. Rehang curtains slightly damp so creases fall out without pressing.
Avoid
Fabric softener on anything, and on microfiber above all — it permanently coats the fibres and kills the electrostatic action. Shaking bedding out before washing, which aerosolizes exactly what you're removing.
Cadence
Bedding weekly. Everything else — curtains, cushion covers, throws, bath mats — monthly.
Separation
Cleaning cloths washed by zone colour, separately from household textiles. Bath mats with bathroom textiles, never with bedding. Dry everything fully before storing.

Electronics & screens Living room

Clean
Keyboards, mice, remotes: 70% alcohol applied to the cloth. Screens: dry flat-weave microfiber only.
Avoid
Spraying any device directly. Alcohol, Windex or anything wet on a screen — it damages the anti-glare coating.
Cadence
Weekly for keyboard and remotes. Monthly for cords, cable runs and behind the monitor.

Fibreglass tub & surround Bathroom Scratches easily

Clean
Dawn on a soft cloth or soft nylon brush. Soap scum: percarbonate solution, 15-minute dwell, soft brush.
Sanitize
Bio Stain, full dwell, rinse.
Avoid
Bar Keeper's Friend. Any scouring powder. Green scrub pads. Acetone or solvents. Very hot water.
Cadence
Weekly. Towel or squeegee after every shower — this does more against mildew than anything applied afterwards.
Why
Gelcoat is a thin surface layer. Once dulled it holds soil, which drives more scrubbing, which dulls it further. The damage compounds and can't be reversed.

Glass — windows & mirrors All rooms

Clean
Interior: Windex Vinegar on flat-weave, buffed dry. Larger panes: dilute Dawn, applicator, squeegee. Full method under Windows & railings.
First
Vacuum the track and sill with the crevice tool before anything wet. Track grit becomes grey paste the moment it's wetted, and it runs down glass you've just cleaned.
Avoid
Cleaning in direct sun — it dries before you can finish and streaks every time. Letting liquid run to the bottom edge of a mirror, where it wicks behind and destroys the silvering. Abrasives or blades.
Spotting
Round marks that survive washing are mineral deposit, not dirt. Dilute citric acid on a cloth, 3–5 min, rinse, dry. If that doesn't shift it, it's etched into the glass and nothing will.

Glass — balcony railings Balcony

Clean
As windows, but damp applicator rather than flooded — no runoff permitted. Work top-down in short sections and dry each before moving on.
Top cap
A genuine high-touch surface. Clean, then Bio Stain with full dwell.
Base channel
The one that matters structurally. Vacuum dry, clean, dry it out completely, keep the weep holes clear. Standing water there sits against the fixings.
Avoid
Leaning over the railing or putting any weight on the glass. If the outboard face can't be reached safely from inside the balcony, it's a building-management job.
Fittings
Stainless clamps and standoffs: with the grain, no bleach, dried after.

Surfaces — Granite to Plastic

Granite counters Kitchen

Clean
Dawn and warm water, then buff dry with flat-weave. On dark polish the buffing is most of the visual result.
Sanitize
70% alcohol. No rinse, no residue, no fragrance.
Avoid
Bar Keeper's Friend — abrasive dulls the polish, oxalic acid strips the sealer. All acids: vinegar, lemon, citric, acidic glass cleaner. Leaving Bio Stain to sit, as the low pH degrades sealer.
Spills
Blot oil, wine and citrus. Don't wipe them across.
Annually
Seal test — pool of water for 15 minutes. If the stone darkens, reseal.

Grout Bathroom

Clean
Detail brush with Dawn. For staining: percarbonate solution, 15-minute dwell, then work with the brush and rinse.
Sanitize
Bio Stain, full dwell.
Avoid
Acidic cleaners on unsealed cement grout — they erode it over time.
Cadence
Monthly. Grout holds soil that tile doesn't.

Laminate flooring Throughout Water sensitive

Clean
Vacuum or dry microfiber first, always. Then damp flat mop, few drops of Dawn in warm water, wrung out hard. Mist the pad, never the floor. Dry behind you.
Sanitize
Not needed. Floors are a dirty zone by definition; cleaning is sufficient.
Avoid
Wet mopping. Steam — it destroys laminate faster than anything else. Oil soaps and polishes, which leave a film that can't be removed. Vinegar. Abrasives.
Why
The core is compressed fibreboard. Swelling at the seams is permanent and unfixable. With no rugs, dust is the dominant soil here — two dry passes beat one damp one.

Light fixtures, upholstered Living · Kitchen

Clean
Switch off at the wall, let cool. Vacuum with the upholstery brush at reduced suction, one direction. In most months that's the whole routine.
Spots
Minimum moisture that will work — barely damp cloth, blotting from outside the mark inward. Don't saturate; tide lines are hard to reverse.
Avoid
Alcohol or any solvent on a fabric shade. Ever.
Note
The kitchen fixture accumulates airborne grease — address it more often rather than more aggressively. Grease aged into fabric doesn't come out. Run the hood while cooking.

Mattress Bedroom

Clean
Vacuum the surface with the upholstery tool. Rotate.
Cadence
Monthly. Protector and pad laundered with the monthly textile cycle.
Note
The risk profile in this room is allergen load, not pathogens. Laundry and vacuuming matter; disinfecting a bedroom achieves nothing.

Paint — walls All rooms Sheens unconfirmed

Flat / matte
Dry microfiber or dry sponge only. Blot marks, never rub. Water burnishes matte paint into a permanent shiny patch, and alcohol lifts the pigment.
Eggshell / satin
Damp microfiber, very dilute Dawn. Light pressure, rinse the cloth, dry at once.
Semi-gloss / gloss
Soap and water freely. Alcohol acceptable on switch plates and door frames.
Always
Test in a low-visibility spot first. Wash bottom-up so streaks don't run into dry paint.
Default
If you don't know the sheen, treat it as matte. That's the safe direction to be wrong in.

Plants, potted Balcony

Weekly
Empty and rinse every saucer — standing water breeds mosquitoes within a week and is the highest-value plant task. Remove fallen leaves from soil and floor. Dust foliage with plain water on a soft cloth. Wipe pots. Lift each pot and clean underneath.
Monthly
Move plants well clear before any washing. Wash saucers with Dawn. Check drainage holes. Return everything onto risers, not flat.
Avoid
Percarbonate, citric acid or any disinfectant reaching soil, foliage or runoff. Leaf shine products. Soap or alcohol on leaves.
Why
The plants are why several products can't be used on this balcony at all — move them first rather than working around them. Dust on foliage genuinely reduces the light a plant gets.

Plastic — toilet seat & lid Bathroom

Clean
Dawn and water on paper towel.
Sanitize
Bio Stain, wipe after contact time.
Avoid
Alcohol — repeated exposure crazes and whitens seat plastics. Abrasives, which scratch and then trap soil permanently. Concentrated bleach, which embrittles the hinges.
Note
Everything touching the toilet is disposable paper towel, straight to the bin. Nothing that touches it touches anything else.

Surfaces — Porcelain to Wood

Porcelain / vitreous china Bathroom

Clean
Dawn daily. Bar Keeper's Friend when needed — this is one of only two surfaces in the apartment that tolerates it.
Sanitize
Bio Stain, full dwell.
Avoid
Little to worry about. Vitreous china is the toughest surface here.

Stainless steel Kitchen

Clean
Dawn daily, always with the grain, never across or in circles. Sink: Bar Keeper's Friend weekly, with the grain, rinsed completely. Rinse and dry after every wash.
Sanitize
Bio Stain, rinsed on food-contact. Alcohol on handles.
Avoid
Chlorine bleach — chloride attacks the passive layer and pits it irreversibly. Steel wool. Abrasive pads. Glass cleaner on brushed finishes. Cast iron or steel items left sitting in the basin, which causes iron-transfer rust spots.
Note
Spotting is standing water, not staining. Drying solves it. Residual Bar Keeper's Friend discolours steel, so rinse thoroughly.

Tile, ceramic Bathroom floor

Clean
Clear hair and debris dry, then Dawn and warm water, then Bio Stain with dwell.
Cadence
Weekly. Grout monthly — see Grout.
Note
Keep the vacuum's main head off the baseboards; use the crevice tool. That's what damaged the MDF previously.

Upholstery Living room Fibre unconfirmed

Clean
Vacuum with the upholstery tool, working the seams — that's where the reservoir is. Covers laundered monthly.
Spots
Blot, don't rub. Minimum moisture. Test any product in a hidden area first.
Cadence
Weekly vacuum, monthly seams and covers.

Window tracks & sills All rooms

Clean
Vacuum with the crevice tool before anything wet. Then Dawn on a damp cloth. Dry the track completely.
Avoid
Wetting a gritty track — it becomes grey paste and runs onto clean glass.
Watch for
Condensation at the base of the glass and in the corners. In a bedroom that's the earliest warning of a humidity problem, and standing water in a track is a mould site.

Wood — interior Kitchen · Living Finish unconfirmed

Cutting boards
Wood by hand, air-dried upright. Plastic to the dishwasher.
Furniture
Dry microfiber. Barely damp with dilute Dawn for marks, dried immediately.
Avoid
Soaking. Oil soaps and silicone polishes, which build a film that can't be removed. Alcohol on any finished wood.

Layout & flow

Where rooms meet matters more than what's in them. Three adjacencies here work against the zoning.

The route

Bedroom → ensuite → living → balcony → kitchen → entry → floors.

Two departures from the standard order, both deliberate. The ensuite is second, not last, because it opens off the bedroom — leaving it to the end means carrying red equipment back through the cleanest room. The entry is last because it opens off the kitchen.

Adjacency 1 — bathroom opens into the bedroom
  • Lid down before flushing. Flushing with the lid up projects aerosol well past the bowl, and here it lands in a sleeping area.
  • Fan during the shower and 15–20 minutes after, door closed. Humidity migrating into the bedroom is what puts mould on the shared wall.
  • Red cloths never cross the threshold. Bath mats launder with bathroom textiles, not bedding.
  • Check the shared wall and the bedroom ceiling corner near the door monthly.
Adjacency 2 — entry opens into the kitchen
  • The most consequential feature of the floorplan: outdoor soil arrives directly into the food-preparation zone.
  • Shoes off at the door, every time. This outperforms every product in the kit.
  • Coarse mat outside for grit, absorbent mat inside for moisture.
  • Shoes enclosed or on a tray. Coats hung, not draped over a kitchen chair.
  • Nothing food-related staged on the entry floor.
Adjacency 3 — balcony opens off the work area, library in the same room
  • Mat inside the balcony door. Balcony footwear stays on the balcony.
  • Vacuum the threshold and track weekly — it's a grit trap directly upstream of the shelves.
  • Door closed on windy days and during balcony sweeping.
  • Vacuum the floor before dusting the shelves, not after.

The library sits downstream of two of the three. Stable humidity around the books matters more than anything applied to them.

Bedroom

Clean zone · green cloths · dry strategy · cleaned first.

Risk profile

Allergen load, not pathogens. Dust mites, skin lipids, textile dust. Laundry and vacuuming matter here; disinfecting achieves nothing.

Supplies — brought in, not stored

Green cloths · alcohol spray · duster · vacuum with brush and crevice tools · flat-weave for glass. Nothing lives in this room; that's what keeps it a clean zone.

Weekly
  1. Strip the bed. Don't shake the bedding out.
  2. Dust top to bottom: fixture, shelf tops, headboard, nightstand, sills, baseboards.
  3. Vacuum including under the bed and along edges with the crevice tool.
  4. Alcohol on handles, switches, nightstand, charger.
  5. Remake.
Monthly
  • Wash the floor — the one wet task here.
  • Mattress vacuumed and rotated; pad and protectors laundered.
  • Curtains laundered and rehung damp.
  • Under-bed and closet floor perimeter.
  • Windows: vacuum track first, then glass, frames, sills.
  • Check the wall shared with the ensuite for early mould.

Ensuite bathroom

Contained zone · red cloths · cleaned second, then closed.

Why second, not last

Standard order assumes a bathroom off a hallway. This one opens off the bedroom, so reaching it last would mean carrying red equipment back through the clean zone. It's done immediately after the bedroom as a sealed unit: gloves on at the threshold, everything reprocessed on exit, hands washed, door closed.

Supplies — permanently resident

The caddy never leaves: Dawn · Bio Stain · Bar Keeper's Friend · soft nylon tub brush · detail brush · toilet brush · red cloths · gloves · paper towel.

Daily
  • Sink wiped, counter cleared.
  • Tub towelled or squeegeed after showering.
  • Fan on during and 20 minutes after, door closed.
  • Lid down before flushing.
  • Towels hung spread out so they dry fully.
Weekly — in this order
  1. Gloves, fan on, surfaces cleared, bath mat out for laundry.
  2. Fixture and mirror. Keep liquid off the mirror's bottom edge.
  3. Shelves, cabinet fronts, tank top.
  4. Sink, tap, counter: Dawn then Bio Stain with dwell.
  5. Tub: soft cloth or soft brush only. Bio Stain, dwell, rinse.
  6. Shower controls, door handle, light switch.
  7. Toilet, second to last. Paper towel only. Bowl, seat, lid, tank, base, floor around it. Bio Stain on the seat, never alcohol.
  8. Floor last, far corner toward the door.
  9. Cloths bagged, brushes drying bristles down, hands washed, door closed.
Monthly
  • Grout with the detail brush; percarbonate where stained.
  • Drain covers off, hook, then Bio-Clean overnight with no water use after.
  • Fan cover washed. Hold a tissue to it to confirm it moves air.
  • Shower head and taps descaled with citric acid, 15 min max.
  • Caulking and baseboard junction inspected — this is where the MDF damage happened.

Living room

Work, studio and library in one space · clean zone · green cloths.

Order within the room

Dust high → vacuum floor → library → work and studio surfaces → touchpoints. The library goes after the floor vacuum so airborne floor dust doesn't settle on books you've just cleaned. This is the one stated exception to top-to-bottom.

Library
  • Weekly: shelf edges and book tops with a dry cloth or duster. That's all.
  • Monthly: books out in sections, dusted spine outward, shelf wiped and dried before they return.
  • Don't pack shelves tight — air needs to move and tight packing damages bindings on removal.
  • Full procedure under Dusting books.
Work area
  • Daily: desk to baseline, one-line handover recorded.
  • Weekly: alcohol on cloth for keyboard, mouse, tablet, chair arms, desk underside edge. Screens dry only.
  • Weekly: vacuum the balcony door threshold and track.
  • Monthly: cords, cable runs, behind the monitor, chair base and castors.
Studio area
  • Daily: neutral baseline — materials cleared, seating reset, one intentional object, lamp lighting.
  • Weekly: dust, vacuum including seams, disinfect visitor touchpoints.
  • Monthly: covers and throws laundered, seams vacuumed, upholstered fixtures with the brush at reduced suction after switching off and cooling.
  • Keep products here fragrance-free.

Kitchen

Blue cloths · highest-priority zone · cleaned after the balcony, before the entry.

Why it outranks the bathroom

The bathroom carries more obvious soil; the kitchen carries higher consequence — foodborne transmission and cross-contamination. It's also the room the entry opens into, so outdoor soil arrives here first.

Daily
  1. Counters cleared, washed, food-contact areas disinfected. Granite buffed dry.
  2. Sink cleared, washed, dried. Treat it as contained, not clean.
  3. Dishes away, nothing soaking. Food sealed. Bin closed.
  4. Table wiped — it's a food-contact surface.
  5. Tap handle and fridge handle: the two highest-touch points in the apartment.
Weekly
  1. Clear counters completely, including the week's accumulation.
  2. Cabinet fronts at hand height, appliance handles, controls.
  3. Cooktop: Dawn on a warm — not hot — surface, soft cloth, scraper near-flat for residue.
  4. Sink with Bar Keeper's Friend, with the grain, rinsed and dried. Drain and strainer separately.
  5. Table and chairs including seat undersides and backs.
  6. Cutting boards. Dishwasher filter. Fridge gasket edge.
  7. Bins washed including the inner rim.
  8. Floor last.
Storage closet — monthly
  • Shelves cleared a section at a time, wiped, dried completely.
  • Check dry goods for pantry moths and weevils. Decanting flour, rice and grains into sealed containers prevents almost all of it.
  • Rotate stock forward. Remove anything expired rather than moving it back.
  • Check the floor and back corners for spills and pest signs.
  • Leave the door open periodically to air it. Humidity in a food store is the actual risk.
Range hood
  • Run it whenever cooking. Airborne grease is what ends up on cabinet fronts and the upholstered fixture.
  • Filter degreased quarterly — hot Dawn soak, or percarbonate for a metal mesh filter. Dry fully.
  • Unconfirmed whether it vents outside or recirculates. If it recirculates it needs a carbon filter on schedule and the grease load everywhere is materially higher.

Entryway

Transition zone · yellow cloths · opens onto the kitchen · cleaned last.

The control that matters

Shoes off at the door, every time. Most tracked-in soil arrives on footwear, and with the kitchen adjacent this single habit outperforms every product in the kit.

Weekly — last in the pass
  1. Mats up. Shake the outside one outdoors; vacuum the inside one.
  2. Vacuum floor, edges, threshold, corners with the crevice tool.
  3. Wash the floor regardless of appearance — the soil here is invisible until it's somewhere else.
  4. Door handle inside and out, deadbolt, switch, intercom.
  5. Shoe tray or rack, and the door at hand height.
Monthly
  • Shoe storage emptied and washed. Shoes cleaned or aired.
  • Coats reviewed, laundered or aired; off-season stored elsewhere.
  • Mats laundered or replaced.
  • Door frame, threshold seal, weather stripping.
  • Wall at hand height — it takes more contact than any other wall here.

Keep the floor clear. Anything sitting on it blocks the weekly wash and becomes permanent within a fortnight. Enclosed shoe storage beats open racks here specifically because of the kitchen.

Balcony

A maintained zone. Painted concrete, extensive glass, wooden bench, potted plants. No drain, neighbours below.

The governing constraint

No drain, and nothing may fall from the sides. You can't wash and rinse. You introduce less water than the surface can hold and extract what you introduce. Everything else follows from this.

Cadence
IntervalWork
DailyTidy the seated area. Dry cloth over bench, side surfaces, glass at hand height. Check saucers.
WeeklySweep, vacuum edges and track, glass, bench, plants, spot clean. No washing.
MonthlyFull wash by section with extraction. Glass both faces. Bench. Pots and saucers.

Weekly sequence
  1. Gloves. Assess for deposits, breakage, standing water.
  2. Any droppings wet first — moisten, cover 5 min, lift, bag, disinfect. Never sweep or vacuum dry.
  3. Sweep with the stiff broom, inward, into a dustpan and bag. Under and behind the plants.
  4. Vacuum the wall junction, door track, glass base channel, bench feet.
  5. Glass — dust dry if gritty, then clean.
  6. Bench wiped with the grain, dried. Check feet and underside.
  7. Plants — saucers emptied and rinsed, dead material removed, leaves dusted, pots wiped and lifted.
  8. Spot clean marks with Dawn on a green cloth. Localized only.

Monthly wash procedure is under Balcony floor, no drain. Algae treatment: half-strength percarbonate, test a hidden corner first, medium nylon brush, plants and bench moved well clear.

Realistic standard

Glass and railings reach indoor standard. The floor won't — gritty painted cement is engineered to trap and hold, because its job is traction. Target: uniform colour, no dark patches, no film, nothing gritty underfoot. Dust returns continuously; the weekly sweep holds it, and that's maintenance working, not failing.

Frigidaire glass-top range

Ceramic cooktop · enamel oven cavity.

Cooktop — weekly
  1. Surface cool, or at most warm. Cleaning hot ceramic can etch it permanently.
  2. Clear debris dry.
  3. Dawn and warm water on a soft cloth; ceramic cream for anything that resists.
  4. Carbonized deposits: dedicated ceramic scraper near-flat, about 30°, on a wet surface. Never dry, never steep, never over the printed markings.
  5. Buff dry, then alcohol as a food-adjacent surface.

Avoid: abrasive powders, scouring pads, ammonia glass cleaner, Bar Keeper's Friend.

The one exception to "cool first"

Sugar spills are scraped off while still hot, with a heat-resistant tool. Sugar bonds to hot ceramic glass and pits it permanently on cooling. This is the one case where waiting causes the damage.

Oven cavity — monthly
  1. Racks out, washed separately. Protect the tub with a towel if washing them there.
  2. Clear carbon flakes from the cavity floor.
  3. Oven cleaner per label, door open, ventilation running. The one kitchen task using paper towel.
  4. Wipe out, then rinse with clear water on fresh towel until no product remains — residue produces fumes on the next heat cycle.
  5. Door glass with Dawn. Keep cleaner out of the vent slots at the door base.

A self-clean cycle is an alternative to chemical cleaning, never a follow-up. Never run it after applying cleaner.

Electrolux refrigerator

Stainless exterior · plastic and glass interior.

Two absolute rules

No alcohol inside — it stress-cracks the liner and dries out the gaskets, and the damage appears weeks later. No bleach on the exterior — chloride pits stainless irreversibly.

Exterior — weekly
  • With the grain, never across or in circles.
  • Dawn on damp microfiber, second cloth with clean water, third dry cloth to buff.
  • Handle disinfected — among the highest-touch points in the kitchen.
  • No abrasive pads, no steel wool, no glass cleaner on brushed finish.
Interior — monthly
  • One shelf at a time so contents stay cold.
  • Glass shelves reach room temperature before washing — cold glass in hot water cracks.
  • Dawn and warm water, or baking soda in warm water for odour. Rinse, dry.
  • Disinfect only where indicated, such as a raw meat leak: wash, Bio Stain, full rinse.
  • Gaskets: fold back the lip, clean the channel with soapy water, dry thoroughly. A dried-out gasket is the usual cause of a poor seal.
  • Crisper drawers out, washed, dried completely.
  • Open box of baking soda, replaced quarterly. Not a spray.
Quarterly
  • Pull out, vacuum condenser coils and the floor beneath with the brush attachment.
  • Drip pan if accessible. Confirm the rear drain hole is clear.

Bosch dishwasher

Stainless interior · weekly filter check, monthly service.

Filter — the most important task
  1. Lower rack out. Twist the cylindrical filter counter-clockwise; lift it with the flat mesh beneath.
  2. Rinse both. Soft brush and a little Dawn on the mesh. Never a wire brush — it damages the mesh and the sealing surface.
  3. Wipe the sump recess clear of debris and glass fragments.
  4. Flat filter first, then lock the cylinder clockwise until it seats. An unseated filter recirculates soil and causes both poor results and odour.
Monthly
  • Spray arms off, jets checked and cleared with a toothpick, refitted, rotating freely.
  • Door gasket and its channel wiped — where biofilm and odour concentrate. Dry after.
  • Dispenser flap and the door edge above the gasket, which the cycle never reaches.
  • Empty hot cycle with citric acid. No bleach in a stainless interior.
Habit

Door ajar after every cycle. Condensation-drying models hold moisture longer than heated-dry, which makes this more important, not less.

Blomberg washing machine

Front loading.

Why this sits upstream of everything

A washer harbouring biofilm recontaminates every textile passed through it — including the cleaning cloths the whole zoning system depends on.

After every load
  • Wipe the gasket dry. Fold back the lip, remove trapped water, lint and objects.
  • Door and dispenser drawer left ajar. Front loaders develop odour and mould at the gasket almost entirely because they're closed while damp.
Weekly
  • Dispenser drawer removed entirely, washed with warm soapy water and a soft brush, dried, refitted.
Monthly
  • Tub-clean or hottest cycle with citric acid, drum empty. No detergent.
  • Drain pump filter at the base — shallow tray and towel first, it holds standing water. Clear, rinse, reseat squarely.
  • Exterior, panel, door glass. Alcohol on handle and controls.
Avoid
  • Excess detergent — the leading cause of residue and odour. Use less than feels right.
  • Bleach on the gasket, which degrades the rubber.
  • Fabric softener on anything.

Front loading. Model type unconfirmed

Every load

Clear the lint filter by hand. This is fire prevention, not performance — the only task in this document with that character.

Monthly
  • Wash the filter in warm soapy water with a soft brush. Softener and dryer sheets leave an invisible film that restricts airflow — this is why a filter that looks clear can still be blocked.
  • Dry the filter completely before refitting.
  • Vacuum the filter housing with the crevice tool.
  • Drum wiped with water, or alcohol if residue is present. Door left open until dry.

Windows & railings

The full method for floor-to-ceiling glass and glass railings, under the no-runoff constraint.

Conditions

Cool, overcast, still day. Not in sun — the solution dries before you can squeegee and streaks every time. Vancouver's default weather is ideal for this.

Tools

Squeegee 12–14 inch plus spare blades · T-bar applicator with microfiber sleeve · waffle-weave towels ×2 · extension pole · two buckets · dilute Dawn · flat-weave cloths · old towels for damming.

The squeegee is what separates "wiped" from "clean". A cloth alone will not get there on large glass.

Before any liquid
  1. Wet-remove any bird deposit or organic matter first. Never dry-brush it.
  2. Vacuum the frames, sill and track with the crevice tool. Skip this and dirty water runs out of the frame onto glass you've just cleaned.
  3. Run a hand over the exterior glass. Gritty → brush it off with a soft brush. Smooth → go straight to washing. Interior faces don't need this.
  4. Lay damp towels along the outer edge and at the base of the balustrade, and across the doorway threshold.
Per pane
  1. Mix weak — about ¼ teaspoon Dawn per gallon of cool water. Far less than feels right; excess soap is the commonest cause of haze and squeegee drag.
  2. Load the applicator, wring it well, cover the pane, then deliberately work the perimeter and corners.
  3. Wipe a dry strip two inches wide across the top with a dry cloth, giving the squeegee a dry start.
  4. Pull straight down in overlapping vertical passes, each overlapping the last by about an inch. Stop about two inches above the bottom rail.
  5. Wipe the blade on a dry towel after every single pass. Not every few — every one. This is the difference.
  6. Finish the bottom strip and the edges by hand with the waffle towel.
  7. Check from an angle and from inside. Streaks are invisible head-on.
Reading the result
SymptomCause
Repeating vertical lineNicked blade or grit on it. Wipe, flip, or replace
Overall hazeToo much soap
Discrete round spotsMineral deposit, not dirt — citric acid
Edge smearsPerimeter not worked before squeegeeing
Railings specifically
  • Both faces. Never lean over the railing or put weight on the glass. If the outboard face can't be reached safely from inside, that's a building job.
  • Outboard face: barely damp cloth, top-down in short sections, each dried immediately. Anything you apply there has nowhere to go but down.
  • Top cap is high-touch — clean then Bio Stain with dwell.
  • Base channel vacuumed dry, cleaned, dried out completely, weep holes clear.
  • Citric acid on spotting: applied on a cloth, never sprayed. Rinse with a damp cloth. Wring into the bucket.

Balcony floor — no drain

The monthly wash, adapted to full containment.

The principle

You can't wash and rinse. Introduce less water than the surface holds, and extract what you introduce. Two consequences: use less soap, because less soap means less rinsing; and work small sections, because a section is only safe while its water hasn't had time to travel.

The enabling tool

A small wet/dry shop vacuum, roughly $60–100. It's the single purchase that makes this achievable — it extracts dirty solution completely, with no puddles, no runoff, no endless wringing. Without it the job is possible but slow and half-hearted.

Also: old towels for damming (six, dedicated) · spray bottle for applying rather than pouring · flat mop you can wring hard · rubber squeegee, whose job here is gathering water inward to a collection point, not moving it to a drain.

One-time: find the slope

On a dry day, pour half a cup of water in the middle and watch where it runs. That direction is where gravity will carry your wash water toward your neighbours, and it tells you where the dams go and which way to gather. If it slopes outward — it probably does — work the outer edge first and move inward, and never leave free water standing on the outer third.

Dam before you start
  • Damp towels along the full outer edge, pressed into the corner where floor meets glass. Damp absorbs; dry lets water run under.
  • A second line at the base of the balustrade channel, since water running down glass exits there.
  • A towel across the doorway threshold — you don't want anything travelling inward onto laminate either.
  • Bucket on the balcony, on a towel. Wring the dams into it as you go. They're the last line of defence, not the plan.
Per section, about one square metre
  1. Everything off the balcony first — plants well clear, bench, mats, furniture.
  2. Sweep dry, and wet-remove any deposits before sweeping.
  3. Mist dilute Dawn from the spray bottle. Damp, not wet. If it pools, that's too much.
  4. Dwell 10 minutes, longer for algae. Mist again if it dries rather than adding more.
  5. Brush in two directions — across, then along.
  6. Extract immediately with the shop vac, or lift with the mop wrung into the bucket. Don't leave a section wet while you move on.
  7. Rinse pass: mist clean water, extract again. One rinse suffices at this concentration.
  8. Next section.
Finish
  • Sanitize the seated area and touchpoints with Bio Stain.
  • Air dry fully before anything returns. Everything back onto risers.
  • Disposal: wash water, rinse water, wrung towels and shop vac contents go down the toilet or the tub — never the kitchen sink. Rinse the tank into the tub and leave it open to dry.
Time

Allow 40–50 minutes for a full monthly floor wash. Extraction is slower than rinsing, and that's the honest trade for containment.

Dusting books

The most conservative procedure here. Monthly, a section at a time.

Never apply liquid of any kind to a book. No alcohol, no soap, no leather dressing without advice specific to that binding.

Method
  1. Vacuum the room's floor first, so you aren't working in disturbed dust.
  2. Take one section off the shelf at a time.
  3. Hold each book closed and firmly shut.
  4. Brush from the spine outward toward the fore-edge, so dust travels away from the text block rather than into it. Soft natural bristles, or the vacuum on lowest setting with fine mesh secured over the nozzle.
  5. Wipe the empty shelf with a barely damp cloth. Let it dry completely before books return.
  6. Return them without packing tight — air needs to move.
Handling
  • Remove a book by gripping the middle of the spine. Hooking the headcap is the commonest cause of damage to older bindings.
  • Keep the collection out of direct sun, off exterior walls, away from the balcony door.
Annually
  • Inspect for foxing, mould odour, insect activity, humidity damage.
  • Isolate anything with active mould or pest damage immediately and assess before further handling.
  • Stable humidity matters more than any cleaning step.

Spills & illness

Escalation, temporary by design.

Spill — blood, vomit, body fluids
  1. Gloves before anything. Ventilate.
  2. Contain the area.
  3. Absorb and remove visible matter with disposable material, working inward from the edges so it isn't spread outward.
  4. Seal in a bag immediately.
  5. Clean with detergent.
  6. Disinfect, visibly wet for the full interval.
  7. Gloves off, hands washed 20 seconds.
  8. Anything reusable that contacted it: laundered hot and separately, or discarded.

Never dry-wipe a dried deposit — wet it first. On laminate, work quickly and dry completely; the water rule still applies during a spill.

Someone unwell
  • Touchpoints from weekly to twice daily: handles, switches, taps, flush handle, fridge handle, kettle, remotes, phone.
  • Bathroom daily rather than weekly, full dwell observed.
  • Hand towels single-user or replaced with paper. Towels laundered daily.
  • Waste bagged and closed before leaving the room.
  • Bedding on the hottest tolerated cycle, not shaken out first.
  • Ventilate every room daily.
  • For gastrointestinal illness, check the Bio Stain label for the specific organism and use the longer dwell it states for that claim.

De-escalate 48 hours after symptoms resolve: one thorough weekly reclean, then back to normal. An indefinite escalation becomes the new baseline and isn't sustainable.

Container discipline
  • Narrow-neck spray bottles rather than buckets for surface solutions — you physically can't double-dip a cloth into a spray bottle, which is the point.
  • Label everything with contents, dilution, date. Refill, wash and relabel monthly.
  • Keep the kit visible and reachable. Anything behind another object won't get used on a low-energy day, and those are the days it exists for.

Safety

Never combine
  • Bleach + ammonia → chloramine gas
  • Bleach + any acid, including vinegar and citric → chlorine gas
  • Bleach + alcohol
  • Hydrogen peroxide + acid

Simplest rule: one product per surface, rinse between. You don't own bleach — keep it that way.

The eight irreversible ones
  1. Standing water or steam on laminate
  2. Abrasives on the fibreglass tub
  3. Bar Keeper's Friend or vinegar on granite
  4. Alcohol inside the fridge or on the toilet seat
  5. Bleach on stainless
  6. Liquid on any book
  7. Percarbonate on the wooden bench
  8. Mixing products
PPE & ventilation
  • Nitrile gloves for all bathroom work, all balcony work, any spill response. Hands washed 20 seconds after removal.
  • N95 for dusty jobs and sanding.
  • Ventilate during oven cleaning, percarbonate work and bathroom disinfection.
  • Alcohol is flammable — keep it away from the range.
  • Never apply any product to skin. Never decant into an unlabelled container.
